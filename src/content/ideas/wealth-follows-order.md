This note treats wealth as a consequence of state, not as a prize that falls from the sky. The strongest line is that money is directed, not accumulated: if the operator is chaotic, cash leaks; if the operator is ordered, money can move through them without friction. That makes this less about manifesting abundance and more about building a trustworthy container for it.

The dragon's sequence is the real mechanism here. Movement only becomes useful when it is disciplined, and discipline only compounds when the surrounding system is orderly. The hound's warning about neglected objects is not about tidiness as aesthetics; it is about stewardship as a signal. A person who cannot keep promises, rooms, tools, or attention in order will struggle to hold bigger responsibilities without leakage.

I also read the griffin's point as a rejection of hoarding. The richest person is not the one sitting on the largest pile, but the one who keeps increasing the value of the environment around them. That is the practical test: can I make my work, my relationships, and my systems more valuable than they were before I touched them?
